## Breaking Down Tax-Saving Options in India

This table shows ways to **reduce your taxable income** under the old tax regime. Here's what each row means:

---

### 🔵 Section 80C — ₹1,50,000 deduction
The most popular tax-saving bucket. You can invest up to ₹1.5 lakh per year in instruments like:
- **PF (Provident Fund)** – deducted from your salary automatically
- **PPF (Public Provident Fund)** – a government savings scheme
- **ELSS (Equity Linked Saving Scheme)** – mutual funds with a 3-year lock-in

All of these count *together* toward the ₹1.5 lakh limit — not separately.

---

### 🔵 Section 80D — ₹25,000–₹50,000 deduction
If you pay **health insurance premiums**, you can deduct:
- Up to **₹25,000** for yourself + family
- Up to **₹50,000** if you're also covering **senior citizen parents**

---

### 🔵 Section 24b — ₹2,00,000 deduction
If you have a **home loan on a self-occupied house**, the **interest** you pay (not the principal) can be deducted up to ₹2 lakh per year. This is one of the biggest single deductions available.

---

### 🔵 Section 80CCD(1B) — ₹50,000 deduction
This is **over and above** 80C. If you contribute to **NPS (National Pension System)**, you get an *additional* ₹50,000 deduction separately. Think of it as a bonus tax-saving slot.

---

### 🔵 Allowance Bills — ~₹94,800/year
Many employers offer **reimbursable allowances** (food coupons, fuel bills, internet, phone). If you **submit actual bills**, these amounts are not taxed. The ~₹94,800 figure is a typical estimate of what most salaried employees can claim this way.

---

### 🧮 Big Picture

If you use **all** of these together:

| Deduction | Amount |
|---|---|
| 80C | ₹1,50,000 |
| 80D | ₹50,000 |
| Section 24b | ₹2,00,000 |
| 80CCD(1B) | ₹50,000 |
| Allowances | ₹94,800 |
| **Total** | **₹5,44,800** |

This ₹5.44 lakh is subtracted from your gross income before tax is calculated — which can **save you ₹1–1.7 lakh in taxes** depending on your tax slab. This only applies under the **old tax regime**.
