---
title: 10 things you should NEVER share at your workplace
category: Corporate
tags: [Corporate]
---

---

**1. Your Salary** — Sharing your salary feels harmless in the moment but it quietly plants seeds of jealousy, resentment, and comparison that are very hard to uproot. Even if you earn less than someone, knowing the difference creates an imbalance in how people see each other. If someone asks, simply say you are happy with your package and focused on growth, it is honest, professional, and closes the conversation without creating awkwardness.

**2. How Close You Live to the Office** — The moment people know you live nearby, you become the most convenient solution to every last minute problem. Late delivery, emergency coverage, quick errand, you will be the first name that comes up because showing up costs you the least in their minds. Protect your time and boundaries by keeping your commute vague, a manageable commute tells them nothing useful.

**3. Your Future Job Plans** — Your career ambitions are yours alone until the right moment. If your manager discovers you are planning to leave or eyeing a different role, you become a flight risk in their mind and flight risks do not get promotions, do not get assigned to important projects, and do not get invested in. Share your career direction only with mentors who have proven they have your best interest at heart.

**4. Complaints About Your Boss** — No matter how frustrating your manager is, the office is never a safe place to voice it. Words travel faster than you expect and often reach exactly the person you were talking about. What feels like a private rant between colleagues can quietly reshape your performance review, your opportunities, and your reputation. Keep those frustrations for trusted friends outside work or write them down somewhere private.

**5. Your Side Hustle** — A side hustle can easily be misread as divided loyalty or lack of commitment to your primary role. Some colleagues out of jealousy or misplaced concern may report it to management, even if it is completely harmless. Unless your contract explicitly allows it and you fully trust the people around you, keep it quiet and keep it separate from your professional identity at work.

**6. Your Dating Life** — Romantic life is one of the fastest spreading topics in any office. A new relationship, a breakup, or any kind of personal drama gets discussed, analyzed, and sometimes weaponized in ways you never anticipated. How people perceive your emotional stability and professionalism can shift based on things that have nothing to do with your actual work. Keep that part of your life firmly outside the office walls.

**7. Time-Wasting Hobbies** — Everyone needs rest and everyone has guilty pleasures, but not everything needs to be shared at work. Mentioning that you spend hours on something that signals low ambition or distraction can subtly change how colleagues and managers perceive your drive and focus. Share hobbies that reflect curiosity, growth, or discipline and keep the rest for your personal life where no one is evaluating you.

**8. Your Secret Shortcuts** — If you have figured out a smarter, faster, or more efficient way to do your work, that is your competitive edge and it took effort and creativity to develop. Sharing it openly risks having someone else take credit for it, or worse, having your manager interpret your efficiency as having too much free time and loading you with more work. Use your advantages quietly and let your results speak.

**9. Personal Struggles** — The office can feel like a community and sometimes it genuinely is, but it is also a professional environment where perception matters enormously. Sharing deep personal struggles may invite genuine sympathy in the moment but that same information can later be used consciously or unconsciously to question your resilience, your focus, or your ability to handle pressure. Reserve your vulnerabilities for people who have earned that level of trust outside of work.

**10. Your True Opinions About Colleagues** — Offices are full of invisible loyalties and unexpected friendships that you may not be aware of. The person you casually criticize might be close friends with the person you are speaking to. Staying neutral, keeping your expressions measured, and never saying anything about a colleague that you would not say to their face is the safest and most professional way to protect both your relationships and your reputation.
---

The core lesson: **maintain clear boundaries between your personal and professional life.** Oversharing might feel natural, but in a corporate environment it can seriously derail your career.
