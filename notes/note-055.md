1. **Observe everything carefully**
  * Notice how things work, how people talk and behave. 
  * This helps you understand the company culture. 
  * You can't fight culture; you can only adapt to it.
  * Who speaks directly vs. indirectly — some people say things clearly while others hint or beat around the bush, noticing this tells you how confident or political someone is
  * Open posture, crossed arms, eye contact, nervous habits — body language often reveals what words hide, someone smiling but with crossed arms may not be as comfortable as they seem
  * How people address seniors, who gets heard in meetings — notice if people use formal titles or first names, and whose opinions are taken seriously vs. ignored
  * Who lunches together, who chats at the coffee machine — informal groupings reveal real relationships and alliances that don't show on any org chart
  * Tight deadlines showing in tone, pace, and facial expressions — stress changes how people communicate, short replies, faster walking, and tense faces are all signals
  * Formal vs. informal language in emails, meetings, and casual chats — someone who writes stiff emails but talks casually in person is adapting to perceived expectations
  * Industry-specific jargon and buzzwords that signal belonging — people use certain words to sound knowledgeable or to fit in with the group culture
  * Who interrupts whom — interrupting is rarely accidental, it often shows who feels more powerful or less patient in a conversation
  * How someone's voice changes with a boss vs. a peer vs. a junior — tone, speed, and confidence often shift depending on who they are talking to
  * Silence, vague answers, or topic-dodging can be telling — what people avoid saying is sometimes more important than what they actually say
  * Who arrives early or stays late and why — some do it out of genuine passion, others due to pressure or to be seen, the reason matters more than the habit
  * Who prepares for meetings, who wings it, who stays quiet — preparation shows respect and seriousness, while silence may mean disengagement or lack of confidence
  * Phones during meetings, constant tab-switching — divided attention reveals how much someone values the meeting or the people in it
  * Feedback given publicly or privately, harshly or kindly — how feedback is delivered says a lot about the culture and the person giving it
  * Personal items on desks reveal personality and priorities — family photos, books, plants, or a bare desk all quietly communicate something about the person
  * Open vs. quiet noise culture — a loud office may signal collaboration or chaos, while a quiet one may mean deep focus or fear of being overheard
  * Formal, casual, or expressive dress code — clothing choices reflect how people see themselves and how seriously they take office norms
  * Blame culture vs. learning culture when mistakes happen — notice if people hide errors or openly discuss them, this reveals psychological safety in the workplace
  * Whether people are appreciated openly or rarely — a simple thank you or public recognition can reveal how healthy and motivated the team culture is
  * Who gets the corner office or window seat — physical space in an office is often a quiet symbol of status and importance
  * Whose ideas get credited in meetings — notice if people build on others' ideas without giving credit, this reveals integrity and politics at play
  * Who is CC'd on important emails — being included or excluded from email chains shows who is trusted, informed, and considered relevant
  * Who gets interrupted and who gets listened to — the people who rarely get cut off usually hold real influence, whether or not their title says so

2. **Don't wait for feedback — ask for it** — The longer you wait, the less you improve. Ask for feedback even if it feels uncomfortable, because most people won't volunteer it unless you directly ask. A simple "what could I have done better?" after a task or meeting can open doors to insights you would never get on your own. The discomfort of asking lasts a second, but the growth from the answer lasts much longer.

3. **Listen more than you speak** — When you listen, you understand others' perspectives. Speaking only broadcasts your own.
  * Here are the occasions when you can ask for feedback:
    * **After completing a task or project** — once you finish something, ask your manager or teammate if the output met expectations and what could be improved next time.
    * **After a meeting or presentation** — ask someone you trust how you came across, whether your points were clear, and if your communication was effective.
    * **After making a mistake** — instead of moving on quietly, ask what went wrong and what a better approach would have looked like.
    * **During one-on-one sessions with your manager** — this is the most natural and safe space to ask how you are performing and where you need to grow.
    * **After receiving a new responsibility** — ask if you handled it the right way and whether your approach matched what was expected.
    * **When you feel stuck or plateaued** — if you feel like you are not growing, ask someone senior what skill or behavior you should work on next.
    * **After a conflict or difficult conversation** — ask a trusted colleague if you handled the situation well or if there was a better way to respond.
    * **During performance review periods** — don't wait for the review itself, ask before it so you have time to act on the feedback.
    * **When you are new to a team or role** — early feedback helps you course correct quickly before bad habits settle in.
    * **After helping someone** — ask if your support was actually useful or if there was something more they needed from you.

4. **Understand company culture** — Fitting into the culture is as important as the quality of your work. A company's culture is the unwritten rulebook that governs how people behave, communicate, and make decisions. You can be the most skilled person in the room, but if you constantly clash with the way things are done, you will always feel like an outsider and your work will be undervalued. Culture shows up in small things — how people greet each other, how meetings are run, how mistakes are handled, and how success is celebrated. The sooner you observe and understand these patterns, the faster you find your place and earn trust. Fitting in does not mean losing yourself, it means knowing when to adapt, when to speak up, and how to work with people the way they naturally operate.
  * **Ways to understand company culture:**
    * **Observe before you react** — spend your first few weeks watching how things work before forming strong opinions or trying to change anything
    * **Notice how people communicate** — is the tone formal or casual, direct or diplomatic, through emails or quick chats
    * **Watch how decisions are made** — are they top-down from leadership or collaborative across the team
    * **See how mistakes are treated** — a blame culture and a learning culture feel completely different and require different behavior from you
    * **Understand what is rewarded** — some companies reward speed, others reward precision, some value visibility and others value quiet consistency
    * **Pay attention to unwritten rules** — things like when people actually arrive, how long lunch really is, or whether it is okay to disagree with a senior openly
    * **Build relationships early** — culture is carried by people, so getting close to the right people helps you understand it faster and navigate it better


5. **Stay out of office politics** — If you get involved, you will eventually become a victim of it. Office politics is the game of using relationships, information, and influence for personal gain rather than collective progress. It feels tempting to join because it seems like how things actually get done, but the moment you step in, you become a player and players eventually get played. Alliances shift, loyalties break, and the person you sided with today may be the one who throws you under the bus tomorrow. The safest and most respected position is to be the person who is known for their work, their honesty, and their neutrality. People trust those who stay above the noise.
  * **How to stay out of office politics:**
    * **Don't take sides in conflicts that are not yours** — when two colleagues or two teams are at odds, stay neutral and focus on the work
    * **Avoid gossip completely** — even if you are just listening, being present in gossip circles associates you with them over time
    * **Don't share sensitive information casually** — what you say in confidence can easily be used against you or someone else later
    * **Be consistent with everyone** — treat your manager, your peers, and your juniors with the same respect so no one can use favoritism against you
    * **Let your work speak louder than your words** — the best defense against politics is a reputation built on results and reliability
    * **Don't complain about people to other people** — if you have an issue, address it directly or let it go, never use others as a venting channel
    * **Recognize political behavior early** — if someone is always flattering seniors, spreading rumors, or creating unnecessary drama, keep a polite distance
    * **If politics is constant and toxic, reassess** — some workplaces are so deeply political that no amount of neutrality protects you, and leaving becomes the healthiest choice

6. **Talk to people in other departments** — Ask what they know that you don't. Most inter-department conflicts happen because people never try to understand each other's perspective. Every department sees the company through a different lens — sales sees the customer, finance sees the numbers, operations sees the process, and tech sees the system. When these perspectives never meet, assumptions fill the gap and assumptions breed misunderstanding, friction, and blame. The person who makes the effort to cross those invisible walls becomes someone everyone values, because they understand the full picture when most people only see their own corner of it. It also makes your work smarter because you stop making decisions in isolation.
  * **How to talk to people in other departments:**
    * **Start with genuine curiosity** — ask what their day looks like, what challenges they face, and what they wish other teams understood about their work
    * **Find natural opportunities** — lunch breaks, office events, shared projects, or even a simple introduction over coffee are all low pressure ways to start
    * **Ask questions that invite insight** — "what do you think we could do better together" or "what do you see from your side that we might be missing" opens real conversations
    * **Share your own perspective too** — it should be an exchange, not an interview, let them understand your world as much as you understand theirs
    * **Collaborate on shared problems** — when a project touches multiple departments, volunteer to be the bridge person who connects the dots
    * **Don't wait for a formal reason** — most people wait for a meeting or a project to talk to other departments, but informal conversations are often more honest and useful
    * **Remember names and what people told you** — following up on a previous conversation shows you were listening and builds genuine connection over time
    * **Use what you learn to improve your own work** — the goal is not just to network but to bring broader thinking into everything you do

7. **Know your colleagues beyond work** — Build genuine human connections. In remote work this is harder, but still necessary. Work relationships that stay purely professional are fragile — they hold up when things are smooth but crack under pressure. When you know someone as a person, not just a job title, you communicate better, trust more, forgive faster, and collaborate with far less friction. A colleague who knows you are going through something difficult will be more patient with you. A teammate you have laughed with will give you honest feedback more comfortably. These human connections are not a distraction from work, they are what make work actually function at a deeper level. In remote settings, this connection does not happen by accident, you have to be intentional about it.
  * **How to know your colleagues beyond work:**
    * **Remember personal details they share** — if someone mentions their kid's school play or a trip they are planning, ask about it later, it shows you actually listened
    * **Be the first to share something personal** — people open up when you open up first, a small personal detail from your side invites them to do the same
    * **Celebrate their milestones** — birthdays, promotions, personal achievements, or even a difficult thing they got through all deserve acknowledgment
    * **Have conversations that are not about work** — talk about books, food, travel, sports, or anything that reveals who they are outside of their job
    * **Show up when it matters** — if a colleague is struggling, a simple check-in message means more than any professional gesture ever could
    * **In remote work, create informal touchpoints** — a quick non-work message, a virtual coffee chat, or a funny meme can do what office small talk does naturally
    * **Don't treat every interaction as transactional** — if you only reach out when you need something, people notice and the relationship stays shallow
    * **Be genuinely interested, not just polite** — there is a difference between asking "how are you" as a formality and actually wanting to know, people can feel that difference instantly**Know your colleagues beyond work** — Build genuine human connections. In remote work this is harder, but still necessary.

8. **Learn first, then create impact** — Especially for young professionals in their 20s: don't try to make an impact before you have learned. Without learning, your work won't be trusted. There is a common pressure on young professionals to prove themselves quickly, to have bold ideas, to suggest changes, and to stand out from day one. But impact built on a shallow foundation does not last. When you jump to solutions before understanding the problem, when you suggest changes before understanding why things are the way they are, you come across as naive rather than impressive. The people who create lasting impact are those who spent time watching, asking, absorbing, and understanding before they spoke. Learning first is not playing it safe, it is playing it smart.
  * **How to learn first and then create impact:**
  * **Spend your first months observing more than talking** — understand how decisions are made, how people work, and what problems actually exist before proposing anything
  * **Ask questions instead of giving answers** — in the early stages, a good question impresses more than a half-baked solution, it shows depth and humility
  * **Learn from people who have been there longer** — they carry years of context that no book or course can give you, tap into that knowledge before assuming you know better
  * **Understand why things are done a certain way** — every process has a history, some are outdated and some are not, but you need to know the difference before suggesting change
  * **Build trust through small reliable actions first** — show up on time, meet your deadlines, do the basics exceptionally well, trust is the currency that makes your ideas worth listening to
  * **Let your learning show in your work** — as you absorb more, your output naturally gets sharper, more relevant, and more aligned with what the organization actually needs
  * **When you do speak up, speak with context** — an idea backed by observation and understanding lands completely differently than one that comes from impatience or assumption
  * **Be patient with the process** — the professionals who are most respected in their 30s and 40s are almost always the ones who were the most curious and humble in their 20s

9. **Carry a notebook to meetings, not a laptop** — Laptops are distracting. Note everything down, then send a summary email to everyone afterward.

10. **Don't evaluate jobs only by salary** — Ask how much your *skills and exposure* will grow, not just how much money you'll make.

11. **Own your work** — Whatever you submit has your name on it. Take full responsibility.

12. **Honor your commitments** — If you commit to something, deliver it on time.

13. **Build genuine relationships at work** — Not just for networking, but because colleagues who become friends will genuinely help you grow. There is a difference between collecting contacts and building connections. Networking for the sake of networking is hollow and people can sense it immediately. But when you invest in someone genuinely, when you show up for them, listen to them, and care about their growth as much as your own, something different happens. Those people become your unofficial mentors, your honest critics, your strongest supporters, and sometimes your closest friends. A genuine work relationship is one where someone will tell you the truth when everyone else tells you what you want to hear, where someone will recommend you not because they have to but because they truly believe in you. That kind of relationship cannot be manufactured, it has to be earned through time, consistency, and authenticity.
  * **How to build genuine relationships at work:**
    * **Be interested, not just interesting** — focus more on understanding the other person than on impressing them, people remember how you made them feel
    * **Show up consistently, not just when you need something** — relationships built only around need are transactions, real ones are built through regular, low stakes presence
    * **Be honest even when it is uncomfortable** — a colleague who gives you real feedback is more valuable than one who always agrees, and being that person for others builds deep trust
    * **Celebrate their wins genuinely** — when a colleague succeeds, be the first to acknowledge it without any trace of jealousy or competition
    * **Support them during difficult times** — checking in when someone is struggling, without any agenda, is one of the most powerful ways to build a lasting bond
    * **Share credit openly** — when a success involves others, say so publicly, people never forget those who gave them recognition they deserved
    * **Have real conversations beyond work** — ask about their life, their dreams, their challenges outside the office, let the relationship exist as a full human connection
    * **Invest in people who are not useful to you right now** — genuine relationships are not strategic, the junior colleague you help today may be the person who changes your career five years from now

14. **Never complain** — Complaining gives away control of your life to others. It signals you have no power over your own situation. Every time you complain, you are essentially announcing that something outside of you is responsible for how you feel and what happens to you. It feels like a release in the moment but it is actually a trap. Complaining keeps your focus on the problem rather than the solution, it drains the energy of people around you, and over time it builds a reputation that follows you everywhere. The person who is always complaining is rarely the person who gets more responsibility, more opportunities, or more respect. People want to be around those who bring energy, not those who drain it. And the deeper truth is that complaining changes nothing, it just makes you more comfortable staying stuck.
  * **How to stop complaining and take control instead:**
    * **Catch yourself before you complain** — pause and ask whether what you are about to say is moving things forward or just venting frustration with no purpose
    * **Replace complaints with questions** — instead of saying "this process is terrible" ask "how can we make this process better", one signals helplessness and the other signals ownership
    * **Separate venting from complaining** — occasionally sharing frustration with a trusted person is human and healthy, but chronic complaining to anyone who will listen is a habit that slowly poisons your mindset
    * **Focus on what you can control** — for every situation that frustrates you, find at least one thing within your power to change or influence and put your energy there
    * **Use dissatisfaction as information** — if something bothers you repeatedly, it is telling you something needs to change, either the situation or your response to it
    * **Build a reputation as a solution bringer** — when you walk into a room known as someone who identifies problems and brings ideas, people listen to you differently than they listen to someone who only points out what is wrong
    * **If something is truly unbearable, act on it** — complaining about a bad situation while staying in it changes nothing, either address it directly, escalate it properly, or make the decision to move on
    * **Practice gratitude as a counter habit** — it is nearly impossible to complain and be genuinely grateful at the same time, training your mind to notice what is working shifts your entire perspective over time

15. **Always be on time** — Be ready before things are supposed to begin, not just at the start time.

16. **Start with trust** — When meeting someone new, assume good intent. Assume they want the company and you to succeed, and help them accordingly.

17. **When in doubt, just ask** — Asking is one of the most powerful things you can do. There is a strange fear that lives in most people around asking questions, a fear of looking ignorant, inexperienced, or unprepared. So instead of asking, people guess, assume, and proceed with half the information they actually need. And then they wonder why things go wrong, why expectations were missed, or why they spent three days working in the wrong direction. Asking is not a sign of weakness, it is a sign of clarity and courage. The person who asks the right question at the right time saves everyone time, avoids costly mistakes, and shows that they care enough about doing things right to set aside their ego. In most workplaces, the people who ask thoughtful questions are not seen as less capable, they are seen as more trustworthy and more serious about their work.
  * **When and how to ask effectively:**
    * **Ask before starting a task** — clarify expectations, deadlines, and desired outcomes upfront so you are not correcting direction halfway through
    * **Ask when something is unclear in a meeting** — if you did not understand something, chances are others did not either, being the one who asks makes you the person who helped the whole room
    * **Ask for the reasoning behind decisions** — understanding why something is done a certain way gives you context that makes you far more effective than someone just following instructions blindly
    * **Ask for help when you are stuck** — sitting stuck for hours to avoid looking like you need help costs far more time than a two minute conversation would have
    * **Ask about priorities when you have too much on your plate** — instead of guessing what matters most, ask your manager directly so your energy goes to the right place
    * **Ask follow up questions** — a single question is good but a thoughtful follow up shows you were listening and thinking, not just collecting a surface level answer
    * **Ask with humility and curiosity** — the tone of your question matters as much as the question itself, approach it as someone who genuinely wants to understand, not as someone challenging or doubting
    * **Ask even when you think you know the answer** — sometimes what you assume and what is actually expected are two completely different things, a quick confirmation can save enormous rework later

18. **Never disrespect anyone** — Regardless of their seniority or your mood, treat everyone with dignity. Respect is not something you give based on someone's title, salary, or usefulness to you. It is a baseline you offer every human being simply because they are human. The way you treat the office cleaner, the security guard, the intern, or the most junior person on the team says far more about your character than how you treat your manager or your most important client. People notice this. They remember it. And in ways you cannot always predict, it comes back to you. A career is long and the world is smaller than it looks. The person you dismissed or spoke down to today may be your colleague, your client, your boss, or your only reference five years from now. But beyond career consequences, disrespect simply corrodes the environment around you and diminishes you as a person far more than it diminishes the person you directed it at.
  * **How to treat everyone with dignity:**
    * **Check your tone before you speak** — especially under stress or frustration, your tone can carry disrespect even when your words do not, slow down and speak with intention
    * **Make eye contact and use people's names** — small gestures of acknowledgment tell someone that you see them as a person, not just a function or a role
    * **Never speak down to anyone** — condescension is one of the most damaging things you can do to a relationship, it closes people off and destroys trust instantly
    * **Apologize when you get it wrong** — if you were short, rude, or dismissive in a moment of stress, go back and acknowledge it, a genuine apology repairs more than most people realize
    * **Treat junior and senior people the same way** — if your respect changes based on someone's position, it is not respect, it is strategy, and people can tell the difference
    * **Do not participate in humiliating others** — whether it is a joke at someone's expense, gossip that degrades someone, or silence while someone is being treated poorly, all of it makes you part of the problem
    * **Respect people's time** — showing up late, canceling last minute, or making someone wait without acknowledgment is a quiet but real form of disrespect that accumulates over time
    * **Remember that everyone is fighting a battle you cannot see** — the colleague who seems slow, the teammate who seems disengaged, or the person who made a mistake may be carrying something heavy, lead with patience before you lead with judgment

19. **Let go of credit** — If you don't get credit for something, let it go. Work done purely for credit is work done to impress and that mindset makes you anxious and ineffective. When your motivation is recognition, every piece of work becomes a performance and every overlooked contribution becomes a wound. You start holding back ideas unless you can be sure they will be attributed to you, you resent colleagues who get praised, and you spend more energy tracking who got credit than actually doing good work. That is an exhausting and shrinking way to live. The people who do the best work over the long run are almost always those who are driven by the work itself, by the problem being solved, by the team succeeding, by something larger than their own name being attached to an outcome. Credit has a way of finding those people eventually, not always immediately, but consistently over time.
  * **How to let go of credit and work from a deeper place:**
    * **Shift your motivation to the work itself** — ask yourself what you are proud of about what you did, independent of whether anyone noticed, that pride is yours and no one can take it
    * **Understand that consistent contribution is visible over time** — one uncredited moment does not erase a pattern of good work, people who pay attention will always notice who is actually doing the heavy lifting
    * **Give credit to others generously** — when you freely acknowledge others contributions, you build a reputation as someone secure and trustworthy, and that reputation is worth far more than any single moment of recognition
    * **Don't make it about the score** — keeping a mental record of who got credit for what turns the workplace into a competition and makes collaboration feel like a threat
    * **Speak up once if it matters, then let it go** — if something was clearly misattributed and it affects your professional standing, it is fair to clarify it calmly and once, but chasing credit repeatedly signals insecurity more than injustice
    * **Focus on building a body of work** — a single project is a moment, but a reputation built over years of reliable and quality output is something no oversight or politics can erase
    * **Recognize that needing credit is a form of dependence** — when your sense of worth comes from external validation, you hand control of your confidence to other people, freeing yourself from that need is one of the most powerful things you can do professionally
    * **Trust that the right people are watching** — managers, mentors, and leaders who are worth working for notice effort and contribution even when they do not always say so out loud, do the work as if they are always watching because the good ones usually are

20. **Acknowledge mistakes and don't repeat them** — Saying sorry matters only when the other person sees you have understood the mistake. Repeating it wastes even the lesson from the first time. An apology without understanding is just a performance. It is words designed to make the discomfort go away without doing the actual work of changing anything. Most people have experienced the hollow feeling of receiving a sorry that shows up again and again for the exact same thing, and they know that after a certain point it stops meaning anything at all. A real acknowledgment of a mistake is not just about saying the right words, it is about showing through your behavior that something actually shifted inside you. That shift is what rebuilds trust. That shift is what makes the other person feel that their experience mattered and that the relationship is worth continuing. Without it, sorry is just noise.
  * **How to acknowledge mistakes and truly learn from them:**
    * **Own it fully and immediately** — do not wait, do not deflect, do not soften it with excuses, the faster and cleaner you own a mistake the more respect you preserve in the moment
    * **Understand it before you apologize** — take a moment to actually grasp what went wrong and why, an apology that comes with genuine understanding lands completely differently than one that is just reflexive
    * **Say what you understood, not just sorry** — instead of only saying sorry, say what you now understand about what happened, this shows the other person that their experience was actually heard
    * **Ask what you can do to make it right** — sometimes an acknowledgment alone is not enough, asking what repair looks like shows that you are willing to go beyond words
    * **Identify the root cause, not just the surface mistake** — if you were late, the mistake is not just lateness, it might be poor planning or overcommitment, fixing the root is what prevents repetition
    * **Create a specific change in behavior** — vague intentions to do better mean nothing, identify one concrete thing you will do differently and actually do it
    * **Track your patterns honestly** — if the same mistake keeps appearing in different forms, it is pointing to something deeper that needs attention, be honest enough with yourself to see that
    * **Understand that repeating a mistake is a choice** — the first time something goes wrong it is often circumstance, the second time it is a signal, the third time it is a pattern and patterns tell people who you really are more than any apology ever could

21. **Work for your own growth, not for approval** — Don't work so people will praise you or love you. Work because it is the right thing for your own development. The praise will follow naturally. When approval is your primary motivation, you become a hostage to other people's opinions. You start making safe choices instead of growth choices, you avoid risks that might invite criticism, and you shape your work around what will be liked rather than what will make you better. The result is a career that looks good on the surface but feels hollow underneath because you are always performing for an audience rather than building something real. Approval seeking is exhausting because it never ends, there is always another person to impress, another standard to meet, another moment where the validation runs out and the anxiety returns. But when you work for your own growth, something settles. You become the authority on your own progress and that is a far more stable and powerful place to operate from.
  * **How to work for growth and not for approval:**
    * **Define what growth means to you personally** — before anyone else's opinion matters, know what you are trying to become and use that as your compass for every decision and every piece of work you do
    * **Measure yourself against your past self, not others** — comparison to others is a game with no finish line, but comparing today's version of you to yesterday's version is always honest and always productive
    * **Do the work even when no one is watching** — the habit you build in private is the character you reveal in public, if you only perform well when there is an audience you are not actually growing
    * **Take on challenges that might make you look bad temporarily** — real growth lives outside your comfort zone and outside your current skill level, approval seeking keeps you in safe territory where no real development happens
    * **Welcome criticism as data, not verdict** — when someone critiques your work, they are giving you information about a gap, take what is useful, discard what is not, and move forward without letting it define your worth
    * **Notice when you are making decisions to impress** — pause and ask whether the choice you are about to make is the one that will actually develop you or just the one that will look best to others
    * **Separate your identity from your output** — your work is something you produce, it is not who you are, when you detach your sense of self from every result you become more resilient and more creative
    * **Trust that genuine growth attracts genuine recognition** — people who are truly developing themselves become visibly better over time and that visibility earns a different kind of respect than applause, it earns trust and that is what actually opens doors
